# 1. Purpose

This document defines the core methodology used by Requira to evaluate software requirements.

It establishes the principles, definitions, and assessment process that guide every AI recommendation generated by the platform.

Unlike implementation documents that describe *how* the system is built, this document defines *how Requira thinks*.

Every AI workflow, audit rule, Requirement Health calculation, and Development Readiness Recommendation should align with the methodology described here.

---

# 2. Core Philosophy

Requira is built on a simple philosophy:

> **Requirements should be reviewed before software is built.**

The platform exists to improve the quality of software requirements by helping teams identify information that may have been overlooked during requirement gathering.

Requira does not replace Business Analysts or Project Managers.

Instead, it acts as an intelligent review partner that highlights areas deserving additional attention before development begins.

---

# 3. What Is a Requirement?

A requirement is any statement that describes what a software system should do, how it should behave, or what constraints it must satisfy.

Requirements may originate from:

* BRDs
* PRDs
* SRS documents
* User Stories
* Meeting Notes
* Discovery Workshops
* Functional Specifications
* Client Documentation

Requira treats all of these as valid sources of requirements.

---

# 4. What Is a Requirement Audit?

A requirement audit is the systematic evaluation of requirement artifacts to determine whether they are sufficiently complete, clear, consistent, and ready for software development.

The objective is **not** to rewrite requirements.

The objective is to assess their quality.

The audit reviews requirements from multiple perspectives, including:

* Completeness
* Clarity
* Consistency
* Risk
* Testability
* Functional coverage
* Non-functional coverage
* Common enterprise software practices

---

# 5. Audit Methodology

Every audit follows the same high-level process.

### Step 1 — Requirement Extraction

Relevant requirements are extracted from uploaded documents.

---

### Step 2 — Requirement Classification

Requirements are grouped into logical business domains such as authentication, payments, reporting, notifications, user management, or non-functional requirements.

---

### Step 3 — Knowledge Retrieval

Relevant requirement engineering knowledge is retrieved from the curated knowledge base.

This provides the context against which uploaded requirements are evaluated.

---

### Step 4 — Requirement Assessment

The extracted requirements are compared against expected software engineering practices to identify potential omissions, ambiguities, and risks.

---

### Step 5 — Finding Generation

The platform produces structured findings with supporting evidence and confidence levels.

---

### Step 6 — Readiness Assessment

Requirement Health and Development Readiness Recommendations are generated based on the overall assessment.

---

# 6. What Is a Finding?

A finding represents an observation made during the audit.

Every finding must satisfy three conditions:

* It is relevant.
* It is explainable.
* It is actionable.

Findings should never be speculative or unsupported.

Each finding includes:

* Title
* Category
* Description
* Supporting Evidence
* Severity
* Confidence
* Recommendation

---

# 7. What Is a Gap?

A gap is an important requirement area that appears to be missing or insufficiently defined within the uploaded artifacts.

Examples include:

* Missing user roles
* Missing permissions
* Missing validations
* Missing notification behavior
* Missing error handling
* Missing integrations
* Missing audit logging
* Missing non-functional requirements

A gap is not automatically considered an error.

It represents an area that should be reviewed by the project team.

---

# 8. What Is a Risk?

A risk is any requirement characteristic that may negatively impact implementation, testing, delivery, or stakeholder expectations if left unresolved.

Examples include:

* Ambiguous wording
* Conflicting statements
* Undefined business rules
* Incomplete workflows
* Missing acceptance criteria
* Missing edge cases

Every identified risk should include an explanation of its potential impact.

---

# 9. Evidence Model

Requira follows an evidence-first approach.

Every recommendation must reference information that supports the assessment.

Evidence may include:

* Specific requirement statements
* Missing expected patterns
* Retrieved knowledge base guidance
* Related requirement context

Recommendations without evidence should not be presented to users.

---

# 10. Confidence Model

Every finding should include a confidence score indicating the system's certainty.

Confidence helps users understand how strongly the available evidence supports the recommendation.

Confidence does **not** indicate correctness.

It represents the strength of the assessment based on available information.

Confidence levels are grouped as:

* High
* Medium
* Low

The exact calculation may evolve as the product matures.

---

# 11. Severity Model

Every finding should be assigned a severity level based on its potential impact.

Severity categories include:

* Critical
* High
* Medium
* Low
* Informational

Severity reflects business impact rather than AI confidence.

For example:

A missing authentication requirement may be **Critical**, even if confidence is moderate.

---

# 12. Requirement Health

Requirement Health is a high-level assessment of the overall quality of the uploaded requirement artifacts.

It summarizes how well the current requirements appear to cover the expected functionality and software engineering considerations.

Requirement Health considers factors such as:

* Functional completeness
* Requirement clarity
* Requirement consistency
* Risk distribution
* Coverage of common requirement areas

The goal is not to produce a perfect numerical score, but to provide a meaningful overview of requirement quality.

---

# 13. Development Readiness Recommendation

Development Readiness is Requira's primary outcome.

It answers the question:

> **"Based on the current requirements, should the project proceed toward development?"**

Possible recommendations include:

* Ready for Development Review
* Needs Further Requirement Review
* Significant Requirement Gaps Identified

This recommendation is always advisory.

Final project decisions remain with the Business Analyst, Product Manager, or Project Manager.

---

# 14. Human-in-the-Loop Philosophy

Requira is designed as a decision-support platform.

The system never:

* Approves projects
* Rejects projects
* Replaces Business Analysts
* Replaces Product Managers
* Makes final project decisions

Instead, it provides structured assessments that help teams make better-informed decisions.

Every recommendation should encourage human review rather than discourage it.

---

# 15. Explainability Principles

Every output produced by Requira should answer four questions:

1. What was identified?
2. Why does it matter?
3. What evidence supports the finding?
4. What should the team consider next?

If any recommendation cannot answer these questions, it should not be presented to the user.

---

# 16. Product Boundaries

Requira intentionally focuses on requirement assessment.

The platform does not:

* Generate complete software requirements.
* Replace requirement gathering sessions.
* Produce project estimates.
* Manage project execution.
* Replace engineering judgment.

Maintaining a narrow focus allows the platform to perform one responsibility exceptionally well.

---

# 17. Methodology Principles

Every future enhancement to Requira should preserve the following principles:

* Review before implementation.
* Evidence before recommendation.
* Explainability before automation.
* Human ownership of decisions.
* Quality over quantity.
* Practical value over technical novelty.
* Continuous improvement through iterative requirement reviews.

These principles define how Requira evaluates requirements and should remain consistent as the platform evolves.
