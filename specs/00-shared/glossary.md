# Purpose

This glossary defines the standard terminology used throughout Requira.

Every specification, implementation document, API, UI, and report should use these definitions consistently. If a term is introduced in future phases, it should be added to this glossary rather than redefined elsewhere.

---

# A

## Analysis

The complete AI-assisted evaluation of one or more uploaded requirement artifacts within a project. An analysis consists of extracted requirements, findings, recommendations, Requirement Health, and a Development Readiness Recommendation.

---

## Audit

The structured process of evaluating requirement artifacts to identify missing information, risks, ambiguities, and opportunities for improvement before software development begins.

---

## Audit Report

A structured document generated after an analysis that summarizes findings, recommendations, Requirement Health, and Development Readiness.

---

# C

## Category

A logical grouping used to organize requirements or findings.

Examples include:

* Authentication
* User Management
* Payments
* Notifications
* Reporting
* Non-Functional Requirements

---

## Clarification Question

A question generated by Requira to help Business Analysts obtain missing or unclear information from stakeholders.

Clarification questions are recommendations, not assumptions.

---

## Confidence

An indication of how strongly the available evidence supports a generated finding or recommendation.

Confidence reflects the quality of available evidence rather than factual certainty.

---

# D

## Development Readiness Recommendation

Requira's advisory assessment indicating whether the uploaded requirement artifacts appear sufficiently prepared for software development.

This recommendation supports human decision-making but never replaces it.

---

## Document

Any uploaded artifact containing software requirements or business information.

Supported examples include:

* BRD
* PRD
* SRS
* FRD
* User Stories
* Meeting Notes
* Functional Specifications

---

# E

## Edge Case

A scenario that falls outside normal system behavior but should still be considered during requirement definition and testing.

---

## Evidence

The information used to justify a finding or recommendation.

Evidence may originate from uploaded documents, retrieved knowledge, or recognized software engineering practices.

Every meaningful finding should be supported by evidence.

---

# F

## Finding

A meaningful observation generated during an audit.

Findings may represent:

* Gaps
* Risks
* Ambiguities
* Improvement opportunities

Every finding should include supporting evidence, severity, confidence, and a recommendation.

---

# G

## Gap

A potentially missing or insufficiently specified requirement area identified during the audit.

Examples include:

* Missing permissions
* Missing validations
* Missing notifications
* Missing error handling

A gap indicates an area requiring review rather than a confirmed defect.

---

# K

## Knowledge Base

The curated collection of requirement engineering guidance, software engineering best practices, and audit frameworks used during the RAG retrieval process.

The knowledge base provides contextual information that supports requirement assessment.

---

# P

## Project

The primary organizational unit within Requira.

A project contains uploaded requirement documents, analyses, findings, and generated reports related to a single software initiative.

---

## Requirement

A statement describing expected system functionality, business behavior, constraints, or quality attributes.

Requirements may be functional or non-functional.

---

## Requirement Artifact

Any document or source that contains software requirements.

Requirement artifacts include formal specifications as well as informal documentation used during requirement gathering.

---

## Requirement Health

A high-level assessment summarizing the overall quality and completeness of the uploaded requirement artifacts.

Requirement Health helps users understand how prepared the project appears before development begins.

---

## Recommendation

An actionable suggestion generated by Requira to help improve requirement quality.

Recommendations should always be supported by evidence and presented as advisory guidance.

---

## Risk

A requirement characteristic that could negatively impact implementation, testing, delivery, or stakeholder expectations if not addressed.

Examples include ambiguity, conflicting statements, incomplete workflows, or missing business rules.

---

# S

## Severity

An indication of the potential business impact associated with a finding.

Standard severity levels include:

* Critical
* High
* Medium
* Low
* Informational

Severity represents business impact, not AI confidence.

---

## Source Document

The original uploaded document from which requirements or evidence were extracted.

---

# T

## Test Scenario

A suggested testing situation generated from the analyzed requirements.

Test scenarios help QA teams validate expected system behavior and identify edge cases.

---

# U

## User Story

A concise description of a software feature written from the perspective of an end user.

User stories may be analyzed individually or as part of larger requirement artifacts.

---

# W

## Workspace

A logical container that groups users, projects, and related resources within Requira.

Workspaces support collaboration and organization but are outside the primary focus of the requirement audit methodology.

---

# Guiding Terminology Rules

The following terminology conventions apply throughout the project:

* Use **Requirement** instead of "specification" when referring to business or functional needs.
* Use **Finding** as the parent term for gaps, risks, and observations.
* Use **Recommendation** instead of "solution" or "fix."
* Use **Development Readiness Recommendation** instead of "approval" or "decision."
* Use **Requirement Health** as the product's primary quality metric.
* Refer to Requira as an **AI-assisted Requirements Readiness Assessment Platform**, not an AI requirement generator or autonomous decision-making system.

Maintaining consistent terminology across specifications, user interfaces, APIs, and reports ensures a unified product language and reduces ambiguity for both users and developers.
